# Exploration Log — Adult / Census Income EDA

This is a narrated record of the actual exploratory journey — what was tried, what it showed, and what that led to next. It is written as the work happens, not reconstructed afterward from the finished report.

## Setup and framing

Downloaded `adult.data` (the 32,561-row train partition) and `adult.names` (the schema/documentation file) directly from the UCI archive. `adult.names` turned out to carry more than a column list — it also states the full 48,842-instance combined file splits into train/test at 32,561/16,281, gives the known class prevalence (23.93%/24.78% `>50K`), and — importantly — describes `fnlwgt` as a CPS sampling weight with a fairly detailed explanation of how the Census Bureau constructs it (raking against state, Hispanic-origin, and race/age/sex population controls). That one detail shaped a framing decision before any analysis code ran: `fnlwgt` is not a demographic attribute of a person, it's an administrative weight, so it gets excluded from substantive analysis and every proportion/rate computed in this project is of the raw 32,561-row sample, not reweighted to population totals. Named explicitly rather than assumed, since getting this wrong would make every prevalence figure quietly misleading.

Frame-the-data determination: multivariate (15 relatable columns), supervised (`income` is a given binary label), and — the one genuinely worth a pause — this data does carry one piece of domain-specific structure a generic multivariate pass would miss: `fnlwgt` marks it as *survey-weighted* data. No pack exists in this skill yet for survey-weighted data, so per protocol that's stated plainly (see above) rather than guessed at, and the analysis proceeds unweighted with that caveat carried through the report.

## Phase 1 — data quality checks

Wrote `R/01_data_quality.R` to load the raw CSV, run the five-part quality checklist, and cache a cleaned Parquet intermediate plus small QC tables for the report to load.

First pass at the numeric range table used `separate(stat, into = c("column", "statistic"), sep = "_(?=[^_]+$)")` to split names like `age_min` / `hours_per_week_n_zero` back into a column name and a statistic name, splitting at the *last* underscore. That broke on the multi-word stat name: `hours_per_week_n_zero` split into `hours_per_week_n` / `zero` instead of `hours_per_week` / `n_zero`, producing a garbled table with phantom `_n` pseudo-columns. Root cause: `across(..., .names = ...)` was left at its default `{.col}_{.fn}` naming, which is genuinely ambiguous once both the column name and the stat name can contain underscores (`hours_per_week`, `n_zero`). Fixed by giving `across()` an explicit `.names = "{.col}__{.fn}"` (double underscore as the deliberate separator) and renaming the stat from `n_zero` to `nzero`, then splitting on the double underscore with `names_sep = "__"` — unambiguous either way. Re-ran and the table came out clean on the second attempt.

Running the checklist surfaced one thing worth a targeted follow-up: `workclass` is missing (`"?"`) on 1,836 rows and `occupation` is missing on 1,843 — close but not identical counts, so the natural question was whether these are the *same* people or not. Cross-tabulating the two missingness flags directly: all 1,836 `workclass`-missing rows are also `occupation`-missing, plus 7 more rows are missing `occupation` alone while still reporting a `workclass`. That's a structural missingness pattern, not scattered noise — it lines up with how the CPS questionnaire skip-logic would behave (someone who doesn't report an employment class has nothing to report an occupation *within*), and it's a meaningfully different story than "5.6% random missingness" would suggest. This becomes a Phase 2 item to characterize further, not just a Phase 1 count.

Also checked the exact-duplicate-row count directly rather than trusting `adult.names`' claim at face value: the documentation states "6 duplicate or conflicting instances," but that figure is for the full 48,842-row `adult.all` combined file. Checking `adult.data` (the 32,561-row train file) on its own found 24 exact duplicate rows — not a contradiction of the documented figure (different denominator), but worth stating explicitly rather than letting a reader assume the "6" figure applies here.

## Phase 2 — general EDA

Ran `R/02_general_eda.R` against the cleaned Parquet intermediate, working through the univariate registry first, then the multivariate registry.

**Distribution shapes** came back mixed rather than uniformly skewed, which matters for how they get charted: `age` and `hours_per_week` are close to symmetric (skew 0.56 and 0.23), `education_num` is essentially symmetric, but `fnlwgt`, `capital_gain`, and `capital_loss` are strongly right-skewed (skew 1.45, 12.0, 4.6 respectively). Digging one level further into `capital_gain`/`capital_loss` — 91.6% and 95.3% of rows sit at exactly zero — showed the skew number alone undersells what's happening: these aren't "a normal-ish distribution with a long tail," they're zero-inflated, with a small, high-value population sitting apart from a mass of true zeros. That distinction matters for Phase 4: a density plot on the full range would just show a spike at zero and nothing else, so the real chart needs to isolate the non-zero population, not fight a badly-scaled axis on the raw variable.

**Outlier characterization** using the standard IQR rule flagged 27.7% of `hours_per_week` as "outliers" (bound at >52.5). That number looked too large to be a real anomaly count, so the next step was to check what was actually driving it: `hours_per_week` is heavily concentrated at exactly 40 (46.7% of all rows report exactly 40 hours), which compresses the IQR band tightly around the mode and makes anything moderately above it — 50, 55, 60 hours, all ordinary full-time-plus schedules — register as a statistical outlier by the mechanical rule. The IQR rule isn't wrong, but reporting "27.7% outliers" without that context would mislead a reader into thinking something unusual is happening for over a quarter of the sample. The actually-unusual tail is much further out: 341 people (1.05%) report 80+ hours/week, which is a genuinely different claim (two full-time jobs' worth of hours, self-reported) worth naming specifically rather than folding into the generic IQR figure.

**Missingness pattern, extended to `native_country`:** cross-tabulating `native_country` missingness against `workclass` missingness showed the two are largely independent — of the 583 rows missing `native_country`, 556 (95.4%) still have a `workclass` reported, meaning almost none of that missingness overlaps with the employment-skip-logic pattern found in Phase 1. That's a second, separate missingness mechanism (most plausibly non-response specifically on the nativity question) rather than a second symptom of the same one — worth keeping distinct in the report rather than lumping "these three columns have missing data" into a single explanation.

**Group comparison, income vs. numeric variables:** ran the mean/std four-quadrant check on all six numeric columns against `income`. Five columns show a clean shift (different mean, and for most, meaningfully different spread too — a compound effect, e.g. `age` 36.8±14.0 for `<=50K` vs. 44.2±10.5 for `>50K`). `fnlwgt` is the exception and — expected, but still checked rather than assumed — it lands squarely in the "similar mean, similar std, no story on its own" quadrant (190,341±106,482 vs. 188,005±102,542). That's actually a useful negative result: it's a direct confirmation that the sampling weight carries no relationship to the outcome, which is what should be true of an administrative weight and supports the earlier decision to exclude it from substantive analysis rather than something asserted without checking.

**Numeric-numeric correlation:** nothing near strong-correlation territory anywhere in the 6×6 matrix (max absolute off-diagonal value 0.148, `education_num` vs. `hours_per_week`). Reportable as a clean null result — no near-redundant numeric variables to worry about downstream.

**Categorical-categorical association:** `marital_status` × `income` came back with by far the strongest association in the set tested (Cramér's V 0.447), well above `sex` × `income` (0.216), `workclass` × `income` (0.164), and `race` × `income` (0.101). That's a flag worth carrying into Phase 3 rather than reporting as a standalone fact: marital status is entangled with age (people are more likely to be currently married at older ages) and with the `relationship` column, so "married people earn more" risks being a confound dressed up as a finding unless age gets checked alongside it.

A `sex` × `relationship` cross-tab, run to sanity-check that `Husband`/`Wife` behave as expected proxies for sex within a marriage, turned up a small but real anomaly: 2 rows code `sex = Male` with `relationship = Wife`, and 1 row codes `sex = Female` with `relationship = Husband`. Only 3 rows out of 32,561 — too small to affect any aggregate statistic — but worth naming rather than silently treating `relationship` as a clean sex proxy, since it's exactly the kind of person a binary husband/wife household-role framing doesn't have a clean box for. Flagged as a Phase 3 candidate rather than dismissed as noise.

`capital_gain` outlier check also surfaced a classic Census-era artifact worth confirming directly rather than leaving as a guess: exactly 159 rows sit at precisely 99,999 (the maximum observed value), a suspiciously round number for organic survey responses to cluster on. Read together with Phase 0's note (once back) on CPS income top-coding practices in this era, this is very likely a top-coding cap rather than 159 people who genuinely reported exactly $99,999 in capital gains.
