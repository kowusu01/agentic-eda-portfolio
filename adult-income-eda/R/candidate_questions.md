# Candidate Questions — Phase 3 Structural Sweep

Generated by stepping through every variable and applying the Habits of Attention.

---

## Categorical variables vs. outcome

**Q1. Education threshold crossing.** At which education level does >$50K become the dominant outcome — and how steep is the jump at the bachelor's degree boundary?
- Data: education_num × income_bin proportions (already computed)
- Pattern: Threshold-Crossing Trend Line
- **Selected for report.** Most interpretable, most actionable — connects directly to any external benchmark on educational wage premium.

**Q2. Occupation ranking as a structural inequality floor.** Across 14 named occupations, the income rate ranges from 0.7% (Priv-house-serv) to 48.4% (Exec-managerial). Is the occupation-level inequality visible as a standalone finding, distinct from education?
- Data: occupation × income rate
- Pattern: Lollipop ranking
- **Selected for report.** The 0.7% floor for private household workers (149 people, zero earning >$50K) is the clearest example of "real people in real conditions" the dataset shows.

**Q3. The gender income gap and what marital status reveals about it.** Female 10.9% vs. Male 30.6% raw. Married female 45.5% vs. Married male 44.6%. The aggregate gap is almost entirely a selection effect.
- Data: sex × marital status × income_bin
- Pattern: Small 2×2 comparison or faceted bar
- **Selected for report.** The finding is surprising and directly corrects a misleading naive reading of the raw numbers.

**Q4. Capital gains as a wealth signal.** 91.7% of the sample has $0 capital gain. Among >$50K earners, 21.4% have any capital gain (vs. 4.2% of lower earners). Capital income is qualitatively different from wage income — it concentrates at the top and is almost invisible below the $50K line.
- Data: income × capital_gain > 0 indicator
- Pattern: Side-by-side proportion bars, or zero-inflation emphasis
- **Selected for report.** The 91.7% zero makes capital_gain the most visually distinctive variable in the dataset; it also has a real-world story (investment access concentrated at higher incomes).

**Q5. Age distribution of high vs. low earners — the narrowing band.** >$50K earners have a tighter age distribution (sd=10.5) centered at 44, vs. ≤$50K earners (sd=14.0) at median 34. High earning is a mid-career phenomenon in this data — not evenly spread across working years.
- Data: age distribution by income_bin
- Pattern: Overlapping density with annotation
- **Selected for report.** The narrower age band for high earners is not obvious from means alone — needs a distribution visual to make the point.

## Questions not selected for main report (with reason)

**Race disparities × occupation/education interaction.** The 2x gap in income rate between White (25.6%) and Black (12.4%) is real and worth naming, but the coarse univariate view is misleading — this requires a conditioning analysis this EDA doesn't have space for without building a partial model. Flagged in anomalies section, not given its own visualization.
- Why not selected: would need occupation × race × income breakdown; the univariate rate is informative but too coarse to be a standalone finding without the conditioning step.

**Self-employed-incorporated advantage.** Self-emp-inc has the highest >$50K rate (55.7%) of any workclass. This is worth noting but doesn't add a distinct visualization angle beyond the occupation ranking.
- Why not selected: absorbed into the occupation/workclass narrative.

**fnlwgt sampling weights and representativeness.** The 120x variation in sampling weights means this sample, treated as unweighted, may over- or under-represent specific demographic cells. Flagged in Technical Notes, not analyzed.
- Why not selected: pursuing weighted analysis would require a different EDA scope; the unweighted caveat is sufficient at this stage.

**Native-country distribution.** 89.6% US-born, then a long tail of countries. The 41 non-US countries each have too few rows for robust income comparisons (Philippines: 198, Germany: 137, down to single-digit countries). Not analyzed further.
- Why not selected: sample sizes too small for most non-US countries to make reliable income rate comparisons.

**Private-household-service workers in depth.** 149 workers, 0% earning >$50K. The extreme at the bottom of the occupational ranking is already named in Q2's lollipop chart. A separate deep-dive on 149 people would be thin.
- Why not selected: captured as the most striking point in the occupation lollipop.

**Hours-per-week distribution by occupation.** Would require a faceted or violin plot across 14 occupations. Informative but adds a second visualization to what is already the hours-per-week angle; too granular for a 4-6 finding report.
- Why not selected: scope and chart count.
